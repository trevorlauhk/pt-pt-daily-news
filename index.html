Sofia loves reading. Every week, she goes to the library near her house. On Monday, she wakes up early and has a quick breakfast. The library opens at nine o'clock, but Sofia always arrives at nine-thirty. Today, Sofia wants to borrow three books. She looks for the books on the shelves and writes down the numbers. The lady smiles and asks, "Have you found the books you want?" Sofia answers, "Yes! I want these three, please." The librarian takes the books and scans them on the computer. Sofia shows her card, and the lady hands her the books. Before leaving, she remembers: "Oh! I also want to return two books." Sofia leaves happily. Now, she’s going home and will start reading the adventure book tonight.

English

In recent years, remote work has become a reality for millions of people worldwide. While it offers unprecedented flexibility, this model raises important questions about balance, mental health, and efficiency. It is undeniable that working from home allows many professionals to organize their day according to their personal needs. However, does this freedom sometimes hide a silent trap? One of the biggest advantages of remote work is, without a doubt, the ability to avoid long and stressful commutes. Consequently, many workers gain precious time, which they can dedicate to family, leisure, or even personal projects. However, this same flexibility requires iron discipline, since the line between professional and personal life becomes increasingly blurred. Some argue that, as long as clear routines are established, teleworking can be as productive—or even more so—than the traditional model. Still, it is crucial that companies implement strategies to ensure their employees do not feel isolated. On the other hand, remote work can also exacerbate inequalities. Not everyone has access to a suitable workspace at home or the technological conditions to perform their duties with quality. Therefore, while this is a valuable solution for many, it is not a universal answer. In short, remote work is here to stay, but it is essential that both employers and employees reflect on how to optimize it. So that this model can truly be beneficial, a balance must be found between autonomy and structure, between freedom and responsibility. After all, what we all want is for remote work to be a choice, not an imposition.
