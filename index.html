English

Yesterday, Pedro went to the City Hall to take care of some documents. He needed a new citizen card because the old one was already worn out and unreadable. Before leaving home, he checked his backpack: he had his ID card, proof of address, and a pen. "Better safe than sorry," he thought. When he arrived at the City Hall, there were many people in line. Pedro felt a little nervous because he didn’t like waiting. So, he took out his phone and started reading the news. After twenty minutes, it was his turn. The clerk was very friendly and explained everything calmly. "You need to fill out this form and then go to the cashier to pay," she said. Pedro filled out the form carefully. Then, he paid the ten euros at the cashier and received a receipt. The clerk said, "The new card will be ready in fifteen days." Pedro thanked her and left happily. On his way home, he stopped at a pastry shop and bought a pastel de nata. "Today was a good day," he thought.

Património em Perigo: Porque Devemos Preservar as Nossas Raízes de Pedra

English

When we walk through the streets of an old city, it’s not just the buildings that speak to us, but the stories they hold. Historical and architectural heritage is, therefore, much more than a collection of stones and mortar: it is the living memory of a people. However, despite its undeniable importance, many of these monuments are at risk, whether due to neglect or economic interests that do not value the past. Since these constructions are unique testimonies of bygone eras, their loss would be irreparable. Imagine, for example, if the Jerónimos Monastery in Lisbon were demolished to make way for a shopping center. We would not only lose a national symbol but also erase centuries of art, faith, and identity. Yet, some argue that preservation is costly and, consequently, not worth investing in something that no longer has practical use. The issue, however, is not just financial. As long as there is political will and collective awareness, it is possible to find creative solutions, such as public-private partnerships or rehabilitation projects that blend the old with the new. After all, so that future generations can understand who we are, it is essential to leave them a tangible legacy. Preserving heritage is not a luxury but a necessity. It’s not just about saving buildings but keeping a nation’s soul alive. May we never have to regret the loss of something we should never have let disappear.
