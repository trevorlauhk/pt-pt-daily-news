English

Yesterday, I went to the City Hall with my mom. We needed to renew her citizen card. We left home early because the bus takes a long time. When we arrived, there were many people in line. My mom said, "Let's wait a bit, but it's not a big deal." After twenty minutes, we reached the counter. The clerk was very friendly. She asked, "How can I help you?" My mom explained that she wanted to renew the card. The lady checked the documents and said, "Everything is fine." Come back next week to pick up the card. Before leaving, we stopped by the stationery store next door. In the end, we went for coffee. Now, we’ll go back on Wednesday to get the card.

English

In recent years, the idea of a four-day workweek has gained traction in several countries, including Portugal. While many argue that this change could improve workers' quality of life, some believe that companies would not be prepared for such a transformation. But is this really the solution to the problems of the modern working world? One of the main advantages highlighted is increased productivity. Studies suggest that, as long as employees have an extra day of rest, they can concentrate better and, consequently, produce more in less time. Furthermore, the reduction of stress and the balance between professional and personal life are benefits that cannot be overlooked. However, not everything is advantageous. In sectors like healthcare or education, where physical presence is essential, implementing this measure could be complicated. Another point to consider is companies' adaptation. Many organizations fear that, in order for productivity not to drop, they would have to increase daily working hours, which, in practice, would nullify the expected benefits. Nevertheless, if well-planned, this change could even attract more talent, since it would offer a competitive edge in the job market. In conclusion, although the four-day week may seem like a distant dream for some, it undeniably brings a necessary debate about the future of work. What is certain is that, for it to work, there must be a joint effort between governments, companies, and workers. After all, the goal is not just to work less, but to work better.
