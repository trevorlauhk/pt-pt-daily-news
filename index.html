Number of citizens denied entry to the EU rose by 7.1% in 2025

According to information from the European statistical service, in 2025, entry to the EU via external borders was denied to 132,600 people, a 7.1% increase compared to 2024 (123,835). Also in the year’s cumulative data, expulsion orders were issued to 491,950 third-country nationals, 5.8% more than the previous year, with Portugal ranking seventh among Member States, with 23,540 orders—a sharp rise compared to the 750 recorded the previous year. France (137,550), Germany (55,240), and Spain (53,700) were the countries that expelled the most foreigners, while Slovakia (500), Estonia (660), and Slovenia (880) were at the opposite end of the table. Furthermore, according to Eurostat, the number of people detected living illegally in a Member State fell by 21.7%, from 918,525 in 2024 to 719,395 in 2025 on average across the EU. Following a departure order, 135,460 people left the EU, 20.9% more than the previous year (112,040), with Germany leading the list of Member States with the most enforced expulsions (29,295), followed by France (14,940) and Sweden (11,250). Among the expelled citizens, the main nationalities were Turkish (13,405), Georgian (10,475), Syrian (8,370), and Albanian (8,020). In Portugal, entry was denied to 2,135 people, 735 people were identified as living illegally in the country, and there were 23,540 expulsion orders, of which 770 were enforced in 2025. "Não há indícios de surto de maior dimensão", mas "trabalho não terminou"

Notícias ao Minuto · PT-PT Daily News

WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus confirmed this Tuesday that there are 11 suspected cases linked to the hantavirus outbreak, nine of which have been confirmed, and two others that will 'certainly' also be considered positive. Although he stressed that 'there is no indication that a larger-scale outbreak will occur,' the official did not rule out that 'the situation may change.'

'These numbers have not changed much over the past week, thanks to the efforts of many governments, and no deaths have been recorded since May 2. [...] All suspected and confirmed cases have been isolated and are being managed under strict medical supervision, thus minimizing the risk of any further transmission. At this time, there is no indication that a larger-scale outbreak will occur,' he said during a joint press conference with Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez.

Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus emphasized that 'it was to be expected that there would be more cases, because […] the first case emerged on April 6, and until it was confirmed that the patient was contagious, around April 24 or 25, there was a lot of interaction among the passengers' on the MV Hondius.

'As you know, the incubation period is six to eight weeks. Due to the interaction while they were still on the ship, even though some preventive measures were taken... it is to be expected that more cases will emerge,' he added.

He reiterated: 'There is no evidence that we are facing the start of a larger-scale outbreak. However, of course, the situation may change, and given the virus's long incubation period, it is possible that we will see more cases in the coming weeks.'

The WHO Director-General also set May 10 as the start of the hantavirus quarantine period, which will last until June 21.

'The recommendation we are making is that there should be strict supervision at home or in a quarantine facility for 42 days, starting from May 10, which takes us to June 21,' he said.

Despite 'hoping' that countries will follow 'the advice and recommendations' of the organization, which 'has clear guidelines,' the official recalled 'the issue of national sovereignty': 'We cannot force countries to apply our protocols. We can only advise and recommend.'

Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus nevertheless indicated that 'anyone showing symptoms will have to be isolated and treated immediately.' In this regard, he underscored that 'the work is not yet finished,' even though the repatriation operation for the Hondius passengers has been declared complete.

The WHO Director-General also reported that all passengers who disembarked from the MV Hondius have been located, including those who left the ship before the operation in the Canary Islands.

'As far as we know, I believe that the passengers who disembarked earlier have already been located. I think there is one in a very remote location, and the United Kingdom managed to locate him through a series of quite complex procedures, and his situation is being closely monitored,' he said, referring to a British national who was found on Tristan da Cunha island.

The official also clarified that he had asked for Spain's help, whose leadership he thanked, because the situation 'exceeded' Cape Verde's capacity.

'The world does not need more selfishness or more fear'

Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez, for his part, considered the disembarkation operation for the MV Hondius passengers 'a success' but assured that 'we will not let our guard down.'

'We will continue to closely monitor the progress of the passengers who are currently hospitalized at Gómez Ulla,' he said.

Sánchez also highlighted that 'the world does not need more selfishness or more fear,' defending the decision to welcome the ship affected by the hantavirus outbreak in the Canary Islands.

'In recent days, we have heard many public officials questioning why the African country of Cape Verde did not host the operation. [...] But for us, it was clear that this was not the issue, that the real question was another. Why wouldn’t we help those in need if we have the means to do so?' he stated.

The head of government also stressed that the operation showed 'the world that Spain is a country committed to health and multilateralism, a country that also helps others whenever it can and when it is necessary.'

'It is our moral duty to the 150 families of tourists and workers who were going through the worst moment of their lives,' he said.

It should be recalled that, to date, three people have died as a result of a hantavirus outbreak that struck the MV Hondius, which was traveling from Argentina across the South Atlantic and triggered an international health alert last weekend.

Hantavirus is generally transmitted from infected rodents. The variant detected on the cruise ship, the Andes hantavirus, is rare and can be transmitted from person to person. The symptoms of infection are initially similar to those of the flu, including cough, fatigue, or headaches and muscle pain. Depending on the strain, it can cause a pulmonary or renal infection.
